PASSAGE 1: The History of the Chicken

The story begins 10,000 years ago in a jungle in Asia and ends today in kitchens all over the world. Chicken is the universal food of our era, crossing cultural boundaries with ease. Until the introduction of large-scale industrial production in the 20th century, the economic and nutritional contribution of chickens was small. Chickens were considered domestic birds that were useful to humanity, but unlike other animals such as the horse or the ox, they did little to change the course of history. Nonetheless, the chicken has inspired contributions to culture, art, cuisine, science and religion.

Recent DNA testing has shown that the chicken's wild ancestor is the red junglefowl. In its habitat from north-eastern India to the Philippines, the red junglefowl looks on the forest floor for insects, seeds and fruit. However, modern chickens inherited at least one trait, their yellow skin, from the grey junglefowl of southern India.

Once chickens were domesticated, they reached Egypt in around 1750 BC as fighting birds and additions to zoos. Drawings decorated royal tombs. Egyptians mastered artificial incubation, freeing hens to lay more eggs. They constructed vast complexes of large rooms (ovens) connected to vents to regulate heat. Around the Mediterranean, chickens were a delicacy among the Romans, who used wine-soaked bread to fatten them. After the fall of Rome, chicken farms vanished and birds returned to their original smaller size.

In the 20th century, the inclusion of antibiotics and vitamins in feed allowed chickens to be raised indoors, leading to factory farming. Today, modern chickens are highly efficient converters of grain into protein, outnumbering humans by nearly three to one.

PASSAGE 2: A Study of Introvert and Extrovert Characters

According to Professor Brian Little, our traits are by no means fixed. In his book Me, Myself and Us, he suggests we adopt "free-traits" to reach goals or "personal projects." We act as "amateur scientists," testing what works. Research by Little and Sanna Balsari-Palsule focused on the workplace, where job fits are rarely perfect.

They found that extroverts initially have advantages in getting noticed. However, when introverts act out of character, they achieve equal performance ratings without negative effects, provided they use "restorative resources"—quiet spaces to recover. Conversely, extroverts find it hard and stressful to rein back their personalities. Younger extroverts may feel like "caged animals" when isolated.

Management is advised to rely less on pigeonholing surveys and instead build environments supportive of free-trait expression, which increases long-term productivity.

PASSAGE 3: Seeing the Colour of Sounds, Hearing the Colour of Numbers

Synesthesia is a condition where senses "leak" into one another (e.g., seeing "five" as green). Synesthetes often keep it secret. John Locke first described it in the 17th century. Recent brain imaging shows that synesthetes' brains light up in vision areas when listening to words. One theory is that we are all born with it, but connections are "pruned" as we grow.

Vilyanur Ramachandran used a test with mirrored 2s and 5s to show synesthetes "see" the color in the physical numbers. However, Daniel Smilek found that the concept of the number is what matters; paper color can interfere with a number color existing only in the subject's head.
